The Cosmological Argument Overview

Exploring the foundations, key thinkers, strengths, and criticisms

Overview

The Cosmological Argument, one of the oldest and most influential arguments for God's existence, posits that the existence of the universe requires an explanation, which it identifies as God. It argues from the existence of the cosmos to a first cause or necessary being.

Key Points

  1. Everything that begins to exist has a cause.
  2. The universe began to exist.
  3. Therefore, the universe has a cause (identified as God).

Philosophers and Thinkers

  • Aristotle (384-322 BCE): Introduced the idea of an "unmoved mover" as the source of all motion in the universe.
  • Al-Ghazali (1058-1111): Formulated the Kalam Cosmological Argument, asserting the universe had a beginning and therefore requires a cause.
  • Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274): Presented the "Five Ways," including:
    1. The Argument from Motion
    2. The Argument from Causation
    3. The Argument from Contingency
    4. The Argument from Degrees of Perfection
    5. The Argument from Design (related to the Teleological Argument)
  • William Lane Craig (1949-present): A contemporary philosopher who revived and defended the Kalam Cosmological Argument.

Strengths

  • Logical structure that appeals to common intuitions about causality
  • Aligns with scientific theories about the beginning of the universe (e.g., the Big Bang theory)
  • Addresses fundamental questions about existence and the nature of reality

Criticisms

  • Infinite Regress: Critics argue that if everything needs a cause, then God would also need a cause. If God doesn't need a cause, why can't the universe itself be uncaused?
  • David Hume (1711-1776):
    • We have no experience of universe creation, so we can't make claims about its cause.
    • The concept of a "necessary being" is incoherent.
  • Bertrand Russell (1872-1970): Suggested that if there can be anything without a cause, it may just as well be the world as God.
  • Quantum Mechanics: Some interpretations suggest not everything requires a cause, challenging a key premise of the argument.
  • Special Pleading: Critics argue that exempting God from the need for a cause is a form of special pleading.

Conclusion

The Cosmological Argument remains a cornerstone of natural theology, addressing deep questions about existence and causality. However, critics contend that it doesn't necessarily lead to the God of classical theism. The debate continues, intersecting with developments in physics, metaphysics, and philosophy.
