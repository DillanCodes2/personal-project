Argument from Consciousness

The Argument from Consciousness suggests that the existence of human consciousness or subjective experiences cannot be fully explained by physical processes alone, and therefore points to the existence of a non-physical mind (God) as its source.

Key Points

  1. Consciousness exists and includes subjective, qualitative experiences (qualia).
  2. Physicalism (the view that everything is physical) cannot fully account for consciousness.
  3. The best explanation for consciousness is a cosmic mind (God).

Philosophers and Thinkers

Thomas Nagel (1937-present)

While not a theist, Nagel's famous paper "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) highlighted the subjective nature of consciousness, which has been used in versions of this argument.

J.P. Moreland (1948-present)

A Christian philosopher who has developed detailed versions of the argument from consciousness.

David Chalmers (1966-present)

Another non-theist whose work on the "hard problem of consciousness" has been influential in discussions of this argument.

Strengths

  • Addresses the mysterious nature of subjective experience.
  • Aligns with many people's intuitions about the non-physical nature of mind.
  • Challenges reductive materialist views of consciousness.

Criticisms

Argument from Ignorance

Critics argue that this is an argument from ignorance—just because we can't currently explain consciousness physically doesn't mean it's inexplicable in principle.

Neuroscientific Progress

Advances in neuroscience continue to shed light on the neural correlates of consciousness, potentially undermining the argument.

The Problem of Other Minds

If consciousness requires a non-physical explanation, it might lead to solipsism (the view that only one's own mind is sure to exist).

Conclusion

The Argument from Consciousness touches on one of the most perplexing issues in philosophy of mind—the nature of subjective experience. While it presents a challenge to purely physicalist views of consciousness, many philosophers and scientists remain unconvinced that it necessitates the existence of God.
