Pascal's Wager

Pascal's Wager, named after the 17th-century French philosopher and mathematician Blaise Pascal, is not an argument for God's existence per se, but rather an argument for belief in God based on practical considerations.

Key Points

  1. If God exists and you believe, you gain infinite reward (heaven).
  2. If God exists and you don't believe, you risk infinite loss (hell).
  3. If God doesn't exist, belief or disbelief has finite consequences.
  4. Therefore, the rational choice is to believe in God.

Pascal, who had a profound religious experience that led him to devote much of his later life to philosophy and theology, didn't intend the Wager as a proof of God's existence, but as a practical argument for belief.

Pascal's Reasoning

Pascal approached the question of belief in God as a kind of decision problem:

  • If you believe in God:
    • If God exists, you gain infinite happiness in heaven.
    • If God doesn't exist, you lose little (perhaps some earthly pleasures).
  • If you don't believe in God:
    • If God exists, you suffer infinite loss in hell.
    • If God doesn't exist, you gain little (perhaps some earthly pleasures).

Given these possibilities, Pascal argued that the rational choice is to believe in God, as it's the option that avoids the worst possible outcome (infinite loss) and offers the possibility of the best outcome (infinite gain).

Strengths

  • Approaches the question of belief from a practical, decision-theoretic perspective.
  • Doesn't rely on evidence for God's existence, but on pragmatic considerations.
  • Appeals to self-interest, which can be persuasive for some.

Criticisms

Many Gods Objection

This argument, raised by philosophers like John Mackie, points out that Pascal's Wager assumes only two possibilities (the Christian God or no God), when in fact there are many religions with different concepts of God.

Doxastic Voluntarism

Philosophers like William Alston have questioned whether belief is something we can choose. If we can't choose our beliefs, then Pascal's advice to "choose" to believe becomes problematic.

Inauthentic Faith

Critics argue that belief based on self-interest rather than genuine conviction would not be viewed favorably by God.

Infinite Expected Value

Mathematicians have pointed out issues with using infinity in expected value calculations, which complicates Pascal's mathematical reasoning.

Assumption of Divine Judgment

The Wager assumes that God would judge people based on belief rather than actions or other criteria.

Conclusion

Pascal's Wager continues to be a topic of discussion in philosophy of religion and decision theory. While it offers an interesting approach to the question of religious belief, it faces significant criticisms. Many philosophers see it as more valuable for the discussions it provokes about the nature of belief, rationality, and religious commitment than as a persuasive argument for belief in God.
